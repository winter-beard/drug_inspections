In Shortage

Cisplatin

The story of how one factory's failure caused a nationwide drug shortage

By Julie Iles

In America, 70% of cancer centers are reporting a shortage of Cisplatin, an oncology drug used particularly in soft tissue cancers and flagged for pediatric use. However it is soft tissue cancers especially, breast, colon and bladder that see some of the highest impacts to mortality risk if treatments are delayed. One medical review estimated a 20% increase in mortality risk with just four weeks of delay.

About 50% of the US' Cisplatin comes from a privately-owned Indian company called Intas Pharmaceuticals, manufacturing both finished injectibles and the active ingredient. Its four FDA-approved facilities in Gujurat India manufacture an estimated 50% of the US market for the drug. The drug has been in shortage before for years, with local manufacturers wanting little to do with the raw platinum compounds, and as one of the most affordable treatments, the profit margin has always been thin. The most recent shortage hit three months after the FDA found massive failures at Intas' newest facility, Pharmez compound near a illage called Matoda. Here 5 million injectible drugs, many of them cancer treatments and 1 billion solid doses of pharmaceuticals are made a year. In a December 2022 report the FDA, suspended the factory for consistently overcounting the viable compounds of the chemotherapy injectible that was present in their cultures. The FDA's investigating officer found the company was destroying evidence of the weight of different samples and different figures were being noted in logbooks, and concluded there was no way to assure the public that previous batches of the drug were at the potency advertised.

ADHD medications too have been highlighted in media reporting. An August 1 joint-letter from the FDA and DEA to Congress mentioned that the dispensing of ADHD-related stimulants has increased 45.5% from 2012 to 2021. Despite a limit on production set by the DEA. 2022 figures found that manufacturers sold only 70% of their quota. "There were approximately 1 billion more doses that they could have produced but did not make or ship. Data for 2023 so far show a similar trend," the letter stated.

Historical Drug Shortages

The average drug shortage lasts 619 days.

However this figure is skewed significantly by a handful of drugs that have been in shortage for more than 10 years.

When compared to larger categories, like anesthetics and antibiotics, ADHD medications historically do not have these kinds of outliers, though the one proprietary drug, also known as Vyvanse appears to have been in shortage longer than most other ADHD-related shortages.

A delay in chemotherapy treatment after surgery of even one month has serious impacts on mortality rates> especially in cases of colon, breast and bladder where a four week-delay saw up to 20% higher mortality rates.

Carboplatin and Cisplatin shortages have previously lasted years.

Cisplatin is most commonly used to treats testicular, ovarian, bladder, head, neck, and lung cancer, while Carboplatin is most often used in ovarian cancer treatment.

Chemotherapy drugs shortages are largely blamed on small profit margins, high production costs and the risk of working with the dangerous Plantinum compounds found in many of the most affordable and popular chemotherapy drugs. However FDA's long focus on remedying these shortages can be seen in the data, as few chemotherapy drug shortages are a long-lasting as some antibiotic or anesthetic ones.
